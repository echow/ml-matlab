Estimating the variance of a distribution

You probably know the formula for the variance of a sample of values. This is distinctly different from estimating the variance of the distribution where the sample comes from. This goes to the heart of the difference between descriptive statistics and inferential statistics. To really emphasize this difference, let's use MATLAB to intuitively grasp the concept of estimating a parameter of a distribution.
Let denote a sample of n values. The mean of these samples is denoted by and is called the sample mean,
.
This term is suggestive of the fact that there is a much larger population of values that we are interested in, and we want to know details of that population, such as its mean. The mean of this population is called the population mean and is denoted by μ. The population mean is also the term that we would use for the mean of the distribution from which we obtain the sample of n values. In the case of a distribution, we can think of the population as containing an infinite number of values! In inferential statistics, the population mean is something that, in practice, we would never know exactly, and there is no formula for it. Thus we approximate it with the sample mean. In descriptive statistics, we assume we have all the members of the population, and in this case, the population mean is just the sample mean where the sample is the entire population.

Example

Consider the Unif(0,1) distribution. The mean of this distribution is 0.5 and the variance is 1/12, but we'll presume these are unknown. Instead, we'll estimate the variance using samples of size 10 from this distribution. We'll repeat this with 5000 samples of size 10, and average the estimates, in order to get a good result. In practice, we would not be afforded 5000 samples, but a single sample that is as large as possible.
First, some definitions.
n = 10; % sample size
ns = 5000; % number of samples
S2 = zeros(ns,1); % array storing the sample variance for each sample
sigma2 = zeros(ns,1); % array storing population variance for each sample
Loop over each sample and estimate the variance in two ways.
for i = 1:ns
x = rand(n,1); % generate sample from the distribution
diff = x - mean(x);
S2(i) = diff'*diff/(n-1); % unbiased estimate
sigma2(i) = diff'*diff/n; % biased estimate
end
Average the estimated variances over the number of samples.
fprintf('average sample variance %f\n', mean(S2));
average sample variance 0.083548
fprintf('average population variance %f\n', mean(sigma2));
average population variance 0.075193
The true value of the variance of the Unif(0,1) distribution is
fprintf('true distribution variance %f\n', 1/12);
true distribution variance 0.083333
The unbiased estimate using the sample variance is a much better estimate. The population variance underestimates the true variance. You can run this script many times to generate different samples each time to see how this affects the estimate of the distribution variance.
