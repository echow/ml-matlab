Student's t-distribution

The Student's t-distribution often arises when the variance is not known and is estimated with an unbiased estimator. This example shows that the distribution of the mean of a sample from a Gaussian distribution follows the t-distribution when the variance of the Gaussian distribution is estimated.

Example

We will plot a histogram of the studentized estimate of the mean of samples from a Gaussian distribution. The histogram can be compared to the probability density functions for the standard normal distribution and the t-distribution.
First we set up some parameters.
vari = 2; % variance of the Gaussian distribution for the samples
n = 10; % sample size
ns = 500000; % number of samples
Loop over each sample and store the estimate.
means = zeros(ns,1); % array storing the estimate for each sample
for i = 1:ns
x = randn(n,1)*sqrt(vari);
S2 = var(x,0); % unbiased estimate of variance
means(i) = mean(x)/sqrt(S2/n); % studentize the mean
end
Compute a standardized histogram. This means that we scale the frequencies so that the histogram can be compared directly to a probability density function.
binwidth = 0.10; % bin width
c = -2.5:binwidth:2.5; % bin centers
f = hist(means,c)/ns/binwidth; % standardize the frequencies
Plot the histogram.
clf
plot(c,f,'o')
Overlay a plot of the t-distribution with degrees of freedom.
hold on
y = tpdf(c,n-1);
plot(c,y,'b-')
Overlay a plot of the standard Gaussian distribution and add a legend.
y = normpdf(c,0,1); % parameters: mean 0, standard deviation 1
plot(c,y,'k:')
legend('histogram','t-distribution','Gaussian dist.')
We observe that the histogram, and thus the estimates of the mean, follow the t-distribution and not the Gaussian distribution, although the distributions are very similar.
